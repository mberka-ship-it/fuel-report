2) Government actions and policy settings

MSO drawdown

Up to 20% of the petrol and diesel MSO has been temporarily released, equal to up to 762 million litres, under the Fuel Security (Temporary Reduction–Securing Regional Supply) Instrument 2026.

Fuel-quality waiver

The temporary fuel-quality change remains active. Government statements say it should add roughly 100 million litres per month of additional petrol supply.

Refinery support

On 20 March, the government said it would amend the Fuel Security Services Payment. The same release says Ampol has confidence to maintain full production and defer planned maintenance.

That refinery-support change matters disproportionately for Queensland because Ampol’s Lytton refinery is Brisbane’s key onshore fuel-security asset. The government’s 20 March release also states that Ampol Brisbane and Viva Geelong together produced 12 billion litres of petrol, diesel and jet fuel in 2025—about 20% of annual Australian needs—and says the government has already expanded diesel storage nationally to over 3.7 billion litres across more than 90 diesel terminals.

Interpretation

Proxy only

These ship-movement signals are useful for direction, not inventory measurement. A tanker in port does not prove quantity unloaded, destination within Australia, or net stock change.

Still, the live port picture does not look like a collapse in physical inflows. The more material deterioration remains in the forward import pipeline: six Australia-bound fuel cargoes due after mid-April have been cancelled or deferred, even though around 74 others are still expected.

Internationally, Reuters reports European and U.S. gasoline cargoes are now heading to Asia, Asian gasoline margins have surged, and Saudi Aramco is supplying April term customers from Yanbu on the Red Sea rather than through Hormuz-exposed routes. That external backdrop is consistent with higher Australian replenishment costs and tighter prompt supply.

4) Brisbane and Queensland storage / supply specifics

There is still no official public daily Brisbane reserve series. The most concrete public Queensland supply datapoints are structural rather than live:

  • Maritime Safety Queensland’s Brisbane port procedures state that Brisbane has an oil refinery and multiple bulk liquid terminals, and that crude oil is one of the port’s principal imports.
  • The government’s 20 March fuel-sovereignty release says Ampol Brisbane is maintaining full production and deferring maintenance.
  • IOR’s Lytton Terminal (a previously verified local storage asset) remains a useful capacity anchor for Brisbane, but it is not a live inventory feed.
Analytical read: Queensland looks better on production resilience than on distribution comfort. Lytton support and active port calls are positives; the absence of daily public inventory data means any Brisbane stock call must remain probabilistic, not exact.

Deep analysis

A. Official stocks are not the current acute problem

The latest official stock data remains above the MSO floor for all three key products. That means today’s most visible pain is not being driven by a documented collapse in nationally measured tank inventories.

B. The current problem is the gap between “fuel exists in the system” and “fuel is cheap and easy to access everywhere”

That gap shows up clearly in today’s data. Queensland port activity remains live, Lytton has policy support to stay at full production, and the government continues to say supply is secure. But retail prices in Brisbane and across Southeast Queensland are at monitored record highs, and the ACCC has already authorised emergency operational coordination among major suppliers. That combination is characteristic of a market with tight prompt supply and stressed distribution, even when national stock figures remain formally adequate.

C. Forward risk has worsened materially

The six cancelled or deferred Australia-bound cargoes due after mid-April matter more than the current tanker picture because they hit the replenishment pipeline, not today’s headline stocks. Reuters also reports tighter Asian refining conditions, Saudi supply being rerouted through Yanbu, and more European/U.S. cargoes heading east. Those are classic signs that Australia’s replacement barrels are becoming more expensive and more operationally complicated to source.

D. What this likely means over the next 1–3 weeks

  • Physical availability: likely remains adequate in aggregate, especially in the large east-coast system, unless there is a second-wave escalation in cancellations or a refinery issue.
  • Retail prices: likely remain elevated and could still move higher before stabilising.
  • Regional / independent supply: remains the softest point of the system.
  • Queensland: comparatively better placed than some regions because it has Lytton plus active import infrastructure, but not immune to allocation stress.
Best concise formulation: Australia’s official stocks are stable; Queensland’s physical supply proxies are active; but forward import risk and live price behavior imply the market remains tight and vulnerable, not comfortable.
