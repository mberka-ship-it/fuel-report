2) Brisbane / Queensland supply proxy

Public daily local reserve numbers do not exist. The local picture therefore has to be inferred from a mix of:

  • QSHIPS / official port-movement access for Queensland ports
  • AIS-derived port pages for recent arrivals, departures, and ships in port
  • Known terminal and refinery capacity, especially around Brisbane / Lytton
  • Retail and wholesale prices, which often tighten faster than official stock datasets update

Today’s strongest Brisbane signal is a same-day crude arrival (STI RAMBLA) with another crude tanker (UNITY VENTURE) still shown in port, while NAVIG8 PROSPERITY has departed. In Gladstone, the live page still shows PAN ASIA and M. PATRICIA CAMILA as LNG tankers in port. Townsville’s expected-call list continues to show fuel-relevant tanker calls over the coming days, including THERESA MICRONESIA on 20 March, GOLDEN LEADER on 22 March, STENA CONTINENT on 28 March, and ATLANTIC T on 30 March.

3) Brisbane-specific storage and infrastructure

Known local capacity exists

The strongest public Brisbane storage datapoint remains the IOR Lytton Terminal, which states that it is a 110 ML diesel import and storage facility at the Port of Brisbane, providing up to 12 additional days of supply storage into the Brisbane market.

Separately, the current sulfur waiver is relevant because it lets additional supply from Ampol’s Lytton refinery stay in the domestic market rather than be exported. Reuters and ABC both reported that this should add roughly 100 million litres per month of extra petrol supply for two months, with an emphasis on regional and spot-market distribution.

Bottom line for Brisbane: there is still no public day-by-day inventory gauge, but the combination of Lytton-linked refining support, known local diesel storage, and visible tanker movements argues against treating Brisbane as a blind spot with no supply visibility at all.

5) How to read inflows and outflows

For this brief, inflows matter more than outflows because the question is short-term domestic supply resilience. That said, Australia does still export hydrocarbons — especially LNG and some crude / condensate — so the country is not literally an inflows-only system.

In practice, the best daily warning indicators are:

  • same-day changes in fuel-relevant tanker arrivals / departures
  • changes in the mix of crude, oil-products, LPG / LNG, and bunkering tanker calls
  • terminal gate prices and retail prices moving faster than official weekly stock data
  • whether official statements say expected shipping continues to arrive on time

Method, caveats, and source hierarchy

  1. Official first: DCCEEW for national stocks and policy settings; ACCC for market scrutiny and weekly price monitoring context; Maritime Safety Queensland / QSHIPS for Queensland movement access.
  2. Daily proxies second: AIS-based public port pages for recent arrivals, departures, ships in port, and expected tanker calls.
  3. Local storage / resilience third: publicly stated terminal and refinery capacity, especially Brisbane / Lytton.
  4. Prices fourth: FuelPrice Australia and PetrolSpy for current retail pricing; company-posted terminal gate prices for spot wholesale signals.

This brief should be read as a decision-support snapshot, not as an audited inventory statement. It is strongest at detecting directional change and early warning signals, especially when official weekly stock data has not yet refreshed.
