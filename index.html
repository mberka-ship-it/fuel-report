Executive judgment

Bottom line: Australia still looks physically supplied rather than physically broken. The official stock base remains above the legal floor, Queensland ports are still moving fuel-relevant ships, and Brisbane retains both refinery and storage infrastructure. 2) Government actions

  • MSO release: up to 20% of petrol and diesel MSO stocks, worth up to 762 million litres, remains available to the market.
  • Fuel-quality adjustments: petrol sulfur flexibility and the 24 March diesel flashpoint adjustment remain in place.
  • Import-support powers: the 28 March package to underwrite additional fuel cargoes remains part of the response.
  • Price relief: fuel excise is halved for three months from 1 April, cutting tax by 26.3 c/L. This helps affordability, not physical stocks.
Forward-looking interpretation: the policy mix now has two tracks — supply continuity (reserve release, standards flexibility, cargo underwriting) and affordability relief (excise cut). That is consistent with a system that is still functioning physically, but under enough stress that price transmission has become politically salient.

4) Brisbane / Queensland supply specifics

  • Brisbane has an oil refinery and multiple bulk liquid terminals, and crude oil is one of the port's principal imports.
  • IOR's Port of Brisbane Lytton Terminal is a 110 ML diesel import and storage facility that can add up to 12 days of diesel supply storage into the Brisbane market.
  • Lytton maintenance has been deferred, which improves near-term operating resilience.
Forward-looking interpretation: Queensland remains better positioned than a purely import-dependent market because it has both refining and storage. That lowers the probability of a sudden hard stop, but it does not immunise the market from high prices, diesel tightness, or prolonged replenishment stress.

Forward-looking analysis

1. The system still looks physically intact. The official stock floor has not been breached in public data, port movements remain active, and Queensland still has both refining and storage infrastructure.

2. Diesel remains the key vulnerability. Diesel has the tightest official margin over the legal floor and the sharpest wholesale and retail pricing pressure.

3. The next risk window is April replenishment. The market is living off a combination of current stocks, current refinery output, and confidence that replacement cargoes will keep arriving. The known deferred or cancelled cargoes matter because they affect replenishment rather than today's visible stock.

4. Policy may lower pain faster than it lowers risk. The excise cut should reduce retail prices mechanically, but it does not improve national stock depth. Cargo underwriting and standards changes do more for physical resilience, but they work with a lag.

5. Base case for the next 2–6 weeks: physical availability likely remains adequate in aggregate, but diesel remains the segment to watch most closely and April cargo execution is the key risk trigger.

Most defensible forward-looking conclusion: Australia's fuel system still looks functional, but it is operating on thinner comfort margins than normal. Queensland should remain supplied, yet the combination of diesel tightness, expensive replacement barrels, and uncertain April replenishment keeps the outlook fragile rather than secure.
