Executive judgment

Bottom line: Australia's official published stock figures still show fuel holdings above the legal minimum. The live Queensland port picture still shows tanker activity and refinery support, so there is no clear evidence of an immediate physical break in supply. The strain is instead showing up in forward import risk, tight distribution, and very high retail and wholesale prices.

2) Government actions and policy settings

  • MSO release: up to 20% of the petrol and diesel MSO has been released, making up to 762 million litres available to the market.
  • Fuel-quality waiver: the temporary fuel-quality measure remains part of the response and Government statements say it should add about 100 million litres per month of extra petrol supply.
  • Refinery support: the Fuel Security Services Payment settings were adjusted on 20 March. The Government says this supports continued operation at Lytton and Geelong.
  • Operationalised release: on 20 March Minister Bowen said more than 500 million litres had already been approved for release and that the two refineries were working full pelt.

4) Brisbane and Queensland storage / supply specifics

  • MSQ's Brisbane port procedures say Brisbane has an oil refinery and multiple bulk liquid terminals, and that crude oil is one of the port's principal imports.
  • IOR says its Lytton Terminal is a 110 ML diesel import and storage facility providing up to 12 additional days of supply storage into the Brisbane market.
  • Minister Bowen's 20 March Brisbane press conference emphasised that the legal stock obligation is designed to hold stock here in Australia, including Brisbane.
Analytical read: Queensland remains better positioned than an import-only region because it has both Lytton refining and Port of Brisbane bulk-liquid infrastructure. But because daily inventory is not public, any Brisbane stock call must remain probabilistic rather than exact.

Deep analysis

A. The official stock story and the lived-market story have separated. Official stocks remain above the legal floor, but that no longer explains the full market experience.

B. Today's problem is best described as tight but functioning. Queensland's live port picture still shows tanker activity, and Bowen said on 20 March that every expected delivery had arrived and that refineries were operating flat out.

C. The real deterioration is in the forward pipeline and replacement cost. The confirmed cancellation or deferral of six cargoes due after mid-April matters more than today's tanker list because it affects replenishment rather than current visible stock.

D. Retail prices are now telling the truth more loudly than the stock tables. Brisbane ULP91 at $2.52/L and diesel at $3.10/L, alongside similar Gold Coast and Sunshine Coast readings, point to a market under heavy replacement-cost and allocation pressure.

E. Near-term implication: physical availability likely remains adequate in aggregate, but prices and local distribution stress can still worsen before official stock tables show obvious deterioration.

Most defensible one-line conclusion: Australia's official stocks remain above the legal floor; Queensland's physical flow proxies remain active; but forward replenishment risk and extreme pricing show the market is still tight, vulnerable, and expensive rather than comfortable.
