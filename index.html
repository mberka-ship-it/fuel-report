What changed since the last brief

Forward-risk worsened. Australia now has confirmation that six fuel shipments due after mid-April were cancelled or deferred, although the minister says about 74 others are still expected and some cancellations have already been replaced by alternative cargoes.
  • No new official DCCEEW weekly stock release since 17 March. The latest formal national stock figures therefore remain unchanged.
  • Queensland immediate supply signals remain active, not broken. Brisbane currently shows TORM GWYNETH and UNITY VENTURE in port, with GRAND WINNER 6 due today; Townsville now shows GOLDEN LEADER in port; Gladstone continues to show tanker traffic, but much of that port’s tanker activity is LNG and therefore not directly usable as a petrol / diesel inflow proxy.
  • Retail stress worsened further. Brisbane ULP91 is now $2.42/L and Brisbane diesel $2.93/L, both at very elevated levels.
  • Refinery support remains a material positive for Queensland. Ampol has deferred Lytton maintenance and is keeping the refinery at full production, improving near-term physical availability even as import risk worsens further out.

Executive judgment

Official measured national stocks: stable / above MSO Immediate Queensland physical availability: tight but still flowing Forward import pipeline beyond mid-April: deteriorating risk Retail and distribution conditions: worsening

The cleanest way to read the market today is this: Australia’s measured national inventories are still above the Minimum Stockholding Obligation and there is no evidence of an immediate national stock collapse. However, the forward import pipeline has clearly worsened with six post-mid-April shipments cancelled or deferred. In Queensland specifically, the better interpretation is “tight but still flowing” rather than “running dry”: Brisbane has crude tankers in port, tanker calls remain visible across Queensland ports, and Lytton is being kept at full production. The most stressed signal remains price, not measured inventory.

4) Brisbane / Queensland storage and supply

What is known directly:

  • There is no public daily Brisbane reserve series.
  • IOR’s Lytton Terminal provides 110 ML of diesel import and storage capacity and says that equates to up to 12 additional days of Brisbane-market diesel storage.
  • Bowen’s refining-support announcement says Ampol can keep Lytton at full production and defer maintenance.

What that means:

Brisbane supply should be read through a four-factor proxy stack: (1) official weekly national stocks, (2) Lytton refinery output, (3) current tanker traffic into Brisbane / Queensland, and (4) retail and wholesale price behavior.

On that proxy stack, Queensland does not look like a market with a broken physical supply chain today. It looks like a market under severe price and distribution stress, with worsening risk further out.

8) Deep analysis — what the signals actually mean

A. The stock picture and the price picture are telling different stories

The measured national stock story is currently stable: the latest official MSO data still show stocks above the statutory minimum and the minister says Australia still has around 38 days of petrol and 30 days of diesel/jet cover. The retail and wholesale price story is the opposite: it is highly stressed, with Brisbane ULP91 at $2.42/L and supplier-posted Brisbane diesel terminal gate prices clustered around the high 280s to low 300s c/L.

B. Queensland looks tight, but not physically broken today

Queensland’s short-run physical picture remains better than the price signal alone would suggest. That is because three positive physical indicators are still present simultaneously:

  • Lytton is being run hard and maintenance has been deferred.
  • Brisbane still has crude tankers in port and another tanker due today.
  • The broader Queensland port network still shows visible tanker movements, especially Townsville and Mackay for products and Brisbane for crude.

C. The forward risk has undeniably worsened

The most important deterioration is not today’s visible tankers; it is the change in the forward schedule. Six shipments due after mid-April have been cancelled or deferred. Even though some have already been replaced and most expected cargoes are still scheduled, that clearly raises the probability of tighter supply if the conflict persists or shipping insurance / routing conditions worsen.

D. Why Gladstone should be handled carefully in a fuel-security model

Gladstone tanker activity is useful as a maritime stress indicator, but it is not a clean petrol/diesel inflow proxy. Gladstone is heavily LNG-oriented and export-heavy. That means a Queensland-wide tanker count can overstate domestic fuel availability if it is not filtered. In other words: Brisbane, Townsville, Mackay and product tankers matter more for east-coast road-fuel availability than total Gladstone tanker counts do.

E. Best current judgment

System-level Australia: stable inventories, worsening forward-risk profile.
Queensland / Brisbane next 7–10 days: tight but still flowing.
Queensland / Brisbane after mid-April: risk profile worse than last week because import cancellations are now confirmed.
Consumer-facing market: worsening, with the strongest evidence being record / near-record pump prices.
